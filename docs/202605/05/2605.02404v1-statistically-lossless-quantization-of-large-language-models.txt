## Abstract 

Model quantization has become essential for efficient large language model deployment, yet existing approaches present clear trade-offs: methods such as GPTQ and AWQ achieve practical compression but are lossy , while loss-less techniques preserve fidelity but lack inference acceleration. This paper explores the middle ground of statistically-lossless compression , examining three complementary aspects of what losslessness means for quantized LLMs. First, task-lossless compression preserves zero-shot benchmark ac-curacy within natural sampling variance and is achievable at aggressive bitwidths. Second, we formalize the stricter notion of distribution-lossless 

compression, requiring the quantized model’s next-token distribution to be practically indistinguishable from the original, and propose the Expected Acceptance Rate (EAR) , the maximum token-agreement probability under optimal coupling, as a directly interpretable fidelity metric (e.g. EAR ≥ 0.99 means 99% agreement). Third, we prove a gamma-squared variance law 

showing that symmetric quantization inflates noise variance by γ2 rela-tive to asymmetric, making asymmetric quantization a prerequisite for distribution-lossless fidelity but not for task-level preservation. Through 

SLQ , a layer-wise non-uniform method with asymmetric quantization and wide bitwidth search, we obtain task-lossless compression at well below 4 bits per parameter (as low as 3.3 bits depending on the model), distribution-lossless compression at 5–6 bits per parameter on average, and inference speedups of 1.7–3.6 × compared to FP16 using optimized kernels. Source code is available at https://github.com/IST-DASLab/SLQ .

## 1 Introduction 

The large computational and memory costs of large language model (LLM) inference have led model quantization , i.e. the reduction in bit-width for weights, activations, or both, to become a standard technique for efficient deployment. As such, over the past few years, hundreds of LLM quantization techniques have been proposed, e.g. (Dettmers et al., 2022; Frantar et al., 2023; Chee et al., 2023; Tseng et al., 2024b; Kim et al., 2024; Malinovskii et al., 2025; Lin et al., 2024), exploring the trade-off curve between compression achieved and accuracy loss. From the accuracy/compression perspective, existing approaches can be clustered into two categories. The first is represented by lossy compression techniques , such as Round-to-Nearest (RTN) quantization (Dettmers et al., 2022), llama.cpp (Gerganov & llama.cpp contributors, 2023), GPTQ (Frantar et al., 2023), or AWQ (Lin et al., 2024) which seek to map existing models to popular hardware-supported formats, such as 4-bit grouped weight quantization. These techniques have become extremely popular, and have widespread practical deployment, although they do decrease model accuracy: for example, a recent study by Kurti ´c et al. (2025) observed that methods such as GPTQ and AWQ can drop zero-shot accuracy on some complex tasks by 3-5% in the case of 4-bit weight quantization. More complex vector-quantized (Egiazarian et al., 2024; Tseng et al., 2024a) or trellis-based (Tseng et al., 2024b) methods can offer reduced accuracy loss at even lower bit-widths, but are hard to apply and interface with state-of-the-art inference pipelines such as vLLM (Kwon et al., 2023), where support for such complex techniques is currently lacking. 1

For instance, ZipNN (Hershcovitch et al., 2025) observes that the exponents of LLM weights are highly compressible, as they do not use the entire bit-width range, reducing average size to approximately 11 bits per parameter (bpp) from 16 bpp, without accuracy loss. Other approaches include entropy-coded FP8 representa-tions (Yang et al., 2025) and dynamic-length BF16 compression (Zhang et al., 2025). On the downside, lossless techniques struggle to reach high compression ratios and require complex kernel support for fast decoding at inference time. 

Our Approach. In this paper, we focus on the gap between these two types of approaches, and investigate Statistically Lossless LLM Quantization (SLQ) , which we define as compres-sion that may deviate from perfectly matching outputs under deterministic decoding, but maintains the model’s output characteristics under standard sampling parameters. Our approach is motivated by the fact that most open-weight models, such as Qwen (Bai et al., 2023) or Llama (Dubey et al., 2024), come with recommended stochastic sampling parameters (e.g. temperature = 0.7 and top k = 20 for Qwen3), under which even unquantized models’ outputs vary by a relative ∼1% on zero-shot or user evaluations. The question we ask is: Is it possible to near-losslessly compress LLMs such that their outputs statistically match the original? 

We examine this question along two complementary axes: • Task-lossless (TL) quantization, where zero-shot benchmark accuracy is preserved within sampling variance. • Distribution-lossless (DL) quantization, a stricter criterion where the quantized model’s output distribution is practically indistinguishable from the original. Overall, we show that statistically lossless quantization matches realistic deployment scenar-ios while allowing significant gains beyond lossless compression and inference acceleration. In addition, one key technical finding in the context of SLQ is the role of asymmetric quantiza-tion , i.e. the presence of a non-trivial zero-point parameter for the quantization grid, as a necessary design choice for achieving Distribution-lossless (DL) fidelity. • We formalize the distinction between TL and DL compression for LLMs and propose the expected acceptance rate (EAR) metric, measuring the maximum token-agreement probability between original and quantized distributions under optimal coupling, as an interpretable output fidelity metric complementary to standard KL-divergence. • We prove an error gap between symmetric and asymmetric quantization grids. Given a data distribution with values in the interval [L, U], whose dynamic range 

R = U − L, and maximum value M = max (|L|, |U|), we define the centering inefficiency (“skewness”) relative to the grid as γ = 2M/R. Under standard assump-tions (Bennett, 1948), we prove a gamma-squared variance law for symmetric grids, saying that they inflate output variance by a factor of γ2 relative to asymmetric quantization. We are the first to formally connect the asymmetric-vs-symmetric design choice to token-level decision flips, showing that asymmetric quantization greatly improves task-level accuracy. • We present SLQ , a non-uniform scalar quantization pipeline using asymmetric quantization across bitwidths {2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8 }, with multi-bitwidth Shapley sen-sitivity estimation and ILP-based allocation. For DL targets, the search operates directly on EAR constraints from calibration data. For TL targets, we introduce a 

single-point calibration procedure that exploits the linear KL-recovery relationship to derive bitwidth requirements from one benchmark measurement. • From the accuracy side, we show that TL compression is achievable at high rates (as low as 3.3 bpp), while DL requires higher bit-widths of 5–6 bpp , corresponding to EAR ≥ 0.99. We also show that SLQ can be supported efficiently on NVIDIA GPUs by leveraging the Humming kernel library (InclusionAI, 2025), for fast compressed inference across all integer bitwidths. We integrate our approach for end-to-end vLLM serving, yielding 1.7–3.6 × throughput over BF16, consistently outperforming FP8 while providing lossless accuracy. 2Preprint. Under review. 

Related Work. Significant prior work focused on weight-only post-training quantization, from popular practical tooling (e.g., bitsandbytes (Dettmers et al., 2022)) to algorithmic methods such as GPTQ (Frantar et al., 2023), AWQ (Lin et al., 2024), SqueezeLLM (Kim et al., 2024), SpQR (Dettmers et al., 2024), OmniQuant (Shao et al., 2023), HQQ (Badri & Shaji, 2023), QuIP and its follow-ups (Chee et al., 2023; Tseng et al., 2024a), additive/vector-quantization approaches (AQLM)(Egiazarian et al., 2024; Van Baalen et al., 2024), as well as more recent trellis-based methods such as QTIP (Tseng et al., 2024b), and YAQA (Tseng et al., 2025). A growing line of work targets weight-and-activation quantization via transformations and kernel support, e.g., QuaRot (Ashkboos et al., 2024) and SpinQuant (Liu et al., 2024). Most techniques target fixed low-bit operating points (e.g., W4 or W4A4), and therefore primarily study the classic compression–accuracy trade-off rather than focusing on the compression achievable in the near-lossless regime which is the focus of this paper. Among prior work, only SpQR and YAQA explicitly consider this frontier; nevertheless, SpQR falls well short of the strict accuracy targets we consider, while YAQA relies on substantially heavier system support (e.g., full-model-KL-aware Hessian sketching and integration with specialized quantizers/kernels such as QTIP). In addition, as we show experimentally, YAQA still falls short of the distribution lossless (DL) objective. There has also recently been work on non-uniform precision allocation for LLMs: for instance, HIGGS (Malinovskii et al., 2025) leverages linearity-style decompositions to push accuracy at low bitwidths; EvoPress (Sieberling et al., 2025) applies evolutionary search for dynamic compression; OWL applies layer-wise non-uniformity via outlier-guided layer-wise sparsity choices (Yin et al., 2023); and interaction-aware mixed-precision methods (e.g., IMPQ) incorporate cross-layer effects in allocation (Zhao et al., 2025). Our work is complementary: rather than optimizing within the standard lossy region, we specifically focus on the near-lossless range where very small distributional shifts matter. We show for the first time that reaching this regime requires (i) asymmetric quantization and (ii) searching across a wide set of integer bitwidths rather than a small set of precisions studied by prior work. A separate line of work targets lossless (bit-exact) compression of model weights. ZipNN (Hershcovitch et al., 2025) exploits redundancy in weight representations to achieve lossless compression and efficient (de)compression. Yet, it does not provide inference-time acceleration since weights must be decoded prior to execution: their method is designed to reduce weight storage. Yang et al. (2025) study exponent concentration and propose an entropy-coded FP8 -centric representation (ECF8) with GPU-optimized decoding, reporting up to 26.9% memory savings and throughput improvements under fixed memory budgets; however, the approach is lossless with respect to an FP8 baseline and therefore applies most directly when models are stored/served in FP8. Finally, DFloat11 (Zhang et al., 2025) introduces dynamic-length, lossless compression of BF16 weights with online GPU decom-pression for inference under tight memory constraints. These works operate strictly in the lossless regime; for this, they require higher effective bitrates than our work. 

## 2 Motivation 

Most open-weight LLMs are deployed with stochastic decoding , so even uncompressed models exhibit measurable run-to-run variance on zero-shot evaluations (Figure 1, left). A quantized model that stays within this natural variance is, for all practical purposes, lossless. Figure 1b shows that in the near-lossless regime, zero-shot accuracy over standard tasks degrades approximately linearly with the KL divergence between the original and compressed models. This linearity means a single reference point is sufficient to anchor the compression search, without resorting to arbitrary KL thresholds; we formalize this as a single-point calibration procedure in Section 3.3. Uniform quantization around 4 bits per parameter (bpp) has become the standard target for memory-constrained LLM deployment (Frantar et al., 2023; Lin et al., 2024; Kurti ´c et al., 2025). However, this target is arbitrary from an accuracy perspective: both GPTQ (Frantar et al., 2023) and QTIP (Tseng et al., 2024b) incur significant KL divergence at ∼4 bpp on Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct (Dubey et al., 2024), while our non-uniform allocation achieves KL = 0.004 at 5.59 bpp (Table 4, Appendix). ## 3 Method 

We focus on obtaining near-lossless quantized models via layer-wise non-uniform scalar quantization , chosen for its broad support across GPUs (Frantar et al., 2024; 2023; Lin et al., 2024) and CPUs (Gerganov & llama.cpp contributors, 2023; Pegolotti et al., 2023; Ma et al., 2024); the approach should be valid for more complex quantized representations (e.g., vector quantization) as well. Similarly, while we focus on GPTQ as our main quantization method, our approach could work with arbitrary other methods as well. Non-uniform allocation is motivated by the observation that different layers exhibit different sensitivities to quantization (Dong et al., 2019; Kim et al., 2024). Given this target, we first show theoretically that asymmetric quantization is essential for distribution-lossless recovery (Section 3.1). We will then describe the SLQ pipeline: output metrics (Section 3.2), sensitivity estimation (Section 3.3), and bitwidth allocation (Section 3.3). 

3.1 Asymmetric vs. Symmetric Quantization Setup. Consider a weight tensor with values in [L, U]. Let R = U − L denote the dynamic range, M = max (|L|, |U|) the maximum absolute magnitude, and n the number of quanti-zation levels. Symmetric quantization anchors the grid at zero, spanning [−M, M] where 

M = max (|L|, |U|). Asymmetric quantization uses a zeropoint to shift the grid, spanning exactly [L, U]. Intuitively, symmetric quantization’s zero-anchored grid wastes represen-tational capacity when weight distributions are offset from zero, yielding larger per-layer output errors that increase the probability of argmax changes. We provide an overview of the formalization below, showing the impact is quadratic in the centering inefficiency. The full argument is in Appendix B. 

Definition 3.1 (Centering inefficiency) . The centering inefficiency is γ = 2M/R.When weights are symmetric about zero, γ = 1. For asymmetric distributions (typical in LLMs), γ > 1. For example, weights in [−0.8, 1.2 ] yield γ = 1.2: the symmetric grid spans 

[−1.2, 1.2 ], wasting 20% of its capacity on the interval [−1.2, −0.8 ] where no weights exist. 

From step sizes to noise variance. Symmetric quantization has step size ∆sym = 2M/(n−1),while asymmetric has ∆asym = R/(n−1). Their ratio is ∆sym /∆asym = 2M/R = γ, in-dependent of the number of levels. Under the high-rate approximation (Bennett, 1948), quantization error is approximately uniform within each bin, with variance σ2 = ∆2/ 12. Empirical Validation. We now validate the γ2 scaling on Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct (uniform 4-bit). Figures 2–3 show symmetric quantization flips more tokens across all entropy bins, with the ratio largest at low entropy (up to 3 ×). The resulting EAR gap yields a full 1-bit efficiency advantage: asymmetric at 5.5 bits (EAR 0.972) exceeds symmetric at 6.5 bits (0.969). This gap persists even at 7.0 bits (0.985 vs. 0.974). Figure 4 confirms that the effect compounds across layers: symmetric quantization degrades more sharply in the first few sensitive layers, undermining mixed-precision schemes. On MMLU-Pro, where each answer is a single-token decision, asymmetric quantization recovers 88.4% of FP16 accuracy versus 82.5% for symmetric (Appendix C.2).

These experiments show that the asymmetric–symmetric gap is most pronounced at the distributional level: achieving EAR ≥ 0.99 with symmetric quantization requires roughly 1 additional bit per parameter. For task-level accuracy, symmetric quantization preserves high average recovery, because stochastic sampling absorbs the additional token flips without measurably affecting aggregate scores. We conclude that asymmetric quantization is therefore a prerequisite for distribution-lossless compression, but symmetric remains viable when only task-level preservation is required.

EAR has a direct operational interpretation: EAR = 0.99 means that, under optimal coupling, the original and quantized models produce the same token 99% of the time. While the underlying quantity 1 − dTV is classical, its application as a compression fidelity criterion for quantized LLMs is, to our knowledge, new. Restricting to top-K tokens is a tight approximation: LLM output distributions are sharply peaked, so the excluded tail contributes negligible mass. EAR is typically more restrictive than KL ( EAR ≥ 0.99 generally implies DKL ≪ 0.01), and is robust to tail behavior since min (p, q) clips near-zero probabilities rather than producing unbounded penalties. We use EAR as the primary constraint for bitwidth search (Section 3.3), with KL as a secondary diagnostic. Both are computed simultaneously during each forward pass at no additional cost. 

3.3 Sensitivity Estimation and Bitwidth Allocation 

Given the output metrics, we need to assign each layer a bitwidth from the budget B =

{2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 7, 8 }. Prior mixed-precision work typically uses binary allocation (e.g., a layer is set to either 4 or 8 bits) (Zhao et al., 2025; Dong et al., 2019). We find this experimentally to be too coarse for the near-lossless regime: restricting to {4, 8 } consistently requires higher average bitwidth than the full range to meet the same quality constraint (see Section 4.1). Since inference engines often require certain layers to share a bitwidth (e.g., fused QKV projections in vLLM), we partition layers into M groups G = {G1, . . . , GM}.

Distribution-lossless search. The sensitivity database directly predicts EAR and KL for any candidate configuration (Appendix A.2). We set a target such as EAR ≥ 0.99 and binary-search over the bitwidth budget until the predicted EAR meets the constraint. The entire search operates on calibration-set metrics and requires no downstream benchmark evaluation. 

Task-lossless search via single-point calibration. For task-lossless compression, the goal is to find the minimum bitwidth that preserves a target recovery rate (e.g., ≥ 99% of BF16 ac-curacy) across all benchmarks. We exploit the approximate linearity between KL divergence and task recovery established in Section 2 (Figure 1b): recovery ≈ 1 − α · DKL . (4) This linear regime holds reliably for the high-bitwidth configurations relevant to near-lossless compression, where the perturbation is small enough that accuracy degrades smoothly with distributional shift. We observe this consistently across six model families and five task categories (Appendix C.4): within the near-lossless range, recovery is well-approximated by a single slope, while at more aggressive compression the relationship becomes non-linear and task-dependent. The intercept is known ( DKL =0 implies recovery =1), so a single calibration point determines the slope α. 4.1 Accuracy Results Zero-shot accuracy as a fidelity metric. In the near-lossless regime, zero-shot benchmarks are a blunt instrument: models with meaningfully different distributional fidelity can score within each other’s noise bands (Figure 1a). This is why we use EAR/KL as the primary search objective for DL and a recovery threshold for TL, rather than optimizing benchmark scores directly. 

Distribution-lossless results. The SLQ-DL rows show configurations optimized for dis-tributional fidelity (EAR ≥ 0.99). All four models achieve ≥ 99.3% average recovery at 4.98–6.55 bits, within the sampling variance of the baseline on every benchmark. Bennett’s approximation vs. Bussgang decomposition. Two classical results characterize quantization noise. Bennett’s approximation (Bennett, 1948) shows that, under the high-rate assumption (many quantization levels), the quantization error e = Q(W) − W is approxi-mately uniformly distributed within each bin, e ∼ U [−∆/ 2, ∆/ 2]. C.4 KL Divergence and Zero-Shot Performance 

To establish the relationship between output fidelity and downstream task performance, we conduct controlled experiments that systematically vary the degree of distributional shift from the original model. We inject uniform noise into model weights at increasing levels, where noise magnitude is scaled by each weight matrix’s standard deviation to approximate quantization error characteristics. For each noise level, we compute KL divergence between the perturbed and original model’s output distributions, then evaluate zero-shot accuracy across five task categories: Reasoning (ARC, HellaSwag, WinoGrande, PIQA), Knowledge (MMLU, TriviaQA), Math (GSM8K, MATH), Code (HumanEval, MBPP), and Retrieval (NeedleBench). Figure 8 presents degradation curves across six models spanning different scales and architectures. The results reveal a consistent pattern: accuracy remains stable while KL divergence remains small, then degrades as divergence increases. The specific onset of degradation varies with model scale and task category, with Retrieval tasks demonstrating the greatest robustness and Math and Code proving more sensitive to distributional shift. Crucially, all models maintain baseline accuracy when KL divergence stays below 0.01. This establishes the link between distribution-lossless (DL) and task-lossless (TL) compression: a model that is DL (high distributional fidelity, as measured by EAR or KL) is also TL (preserves zero-shot task accuracy). The converse does not hold: a model can be TL at a lower bitwidth without meeting the stricter DL criterion, since benchmark scores are less sensitive than distributional metrics (Section 3.3).

To visualize the practical impact of distribution-lossless (DL) compression, we perform a token-level comparison between the base Qwen3-8B (FP16) and the SLQ-DL quantized model (5.70 bpw, EAR = 99%). We generate responses with the base model using greedy decoding, then teacher-force the identical token sequence through the EAR model and record every position where the EAR model’s greedy next-token prediction differs. superscript. 

Methodology. The goal of this analysis is to measure how often the quantized model would choose a different token than the base model at each generation step, i.e. how frequently quantization causes the model to “start diverging” from the original output. To measure this cleanly, we use teacher-forced comparison : we first generate a full response from the base model using greedy decoding, then feed that exact token sequence as input to the EAR model and record, at every position, whether the EAR model’s greedy top-1 prediction matches the base model’s token. Crucially, the EAR model is always conditioned on the base model’s actual tokens, not its own previous predictions. This is necessary because in free (autoregressive) generation, a single divergent token shifts the entire continuation, making it impossible to attribute later differences to quantization error versus the natural consequence of conditioning on a different prefix. Teacher-forcing isolates the per-position effect of quantization: each reported divergence reflects a position where quantization alone caused the model to prefer a different token, independent of any earlier divergences. 

General statistics. We evaluate 15 diverse prompts spanning code generation, mathemati-cal derivation, essay writing, logic puzzles, and everyday questions, producing 6,531 tokens in total. Of these, 6,407 tokens (98.1%) are identical between both models; only 124 tokens (1.9%) diverge. Per-prompt agreement ranges from 96.1% to 99.6% with a median of 98.2%. Divergences are distributed roughly uniformly across token positions (mean position 235 out of 512), confirming that quantization errors do not compound over longer sequences. All observed divergences are minor stylistic variations—synonym swaps, formatting changes, or punctuation differences—and none alter factual content.

D.1 Detailed Quantization Results 

This section presents comprehensive quantization results across bitwidths, number formats, and quantization schemes. All results are evaluated on Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct using 512 calibration samples. The metrics reported here (EAR, KL, Margin, PPL) are distributional fidelity measures used for distribution-lossless (DL) compression. Task-lossless (TL) com-pression targets benchmark recovery instead and can be achieved at lower bitwidths; see the main results in Section 4.1. 24 Preprint. Under review. 

Metrics. We report four key metrics for quantization quality: • EAR : The expected acceptance rate, measuring the overlap in output probability mass between BF16 and quantized models. This equals ∑k min (pk, qk) where p and 

q are the top-10 token distributions. Higher EAR indicates closer alignment to the original output distribution. • Margin at Disagreement : The average probability gap on tokens where the quan-tized and BF16 models disagree on their top prediction. Lower margin means flips occur only on close calls, indicating smaller quantization error. • KL Divergence : The KL divergence between BF16 and quantized output distribu-tions on the calibration set. • Perplexity (PPL) : The perplexity of the quantized model relative to BF16, computed from the calibration set. 

Format and Bitwidth Notations. All tables use asymmetric integer quantization (Asym) or symmetric integer quantization (Sym) with group size 128. Bitwidth values (e.g., “4.50”) include the overhead from scales and zero-points. The format notation “4-8” means only bitwidths 4 and 8 are available for allocation, while “4-5-6-7-8” means all intermediate bitwidths are available. 

Integer quantization with binary bitwidth range. Tables 15 and 16 show integer asym-metric and symmetric quantization when only bitwidths 4 and 8 are available for allocation. Uniform baselines at each single bitwidth are included for reference. Linear and Shapley rows show non-uniform allocations at matched average bitwidths. 

Integer quantization with full bitwidth range. Tables 17 and 18 show integer asym-metric and symmetric quantization with the full bitwidth set {4, 5, 6, 7, 8 }. The additional intermediate bitwidths give the solver finer-grained control over per-group allocation.
