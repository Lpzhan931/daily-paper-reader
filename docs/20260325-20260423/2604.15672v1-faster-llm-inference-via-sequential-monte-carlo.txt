## Abstract 

Speculative decoding (SD) accelerates language model inference by drafting tokens from a cheap proposal model and verifying them against an expensive target model via rejection sampling. Because rejection truncates the draft block at the first error, throughput degrades when draft and target diverge. Rather than rejecting draft tokens outright, we propose to reweight 

them. To this end, we introduce sequential Monte Carlo speculative decoding (SMC-SD), which replaces token-level rejection with importance-weighted resampling over a population of draft particles. SMC-SD is a principled approximate inference scheme that trades exactness for additional speed, while preserving theoretical bounds on its per-step approximation error. Because LLM inference is memory bandwidth-bound, the arithmetic needed to draft particles and to score them in parallel comes nearly for free—SMC-SD uses idle compute to turn verification into a vectorized, fixed-size operation with no rollback. Empirically, SMC-SD achieves 2.36 × speed-up over speculative decoding and a 5.2 × speed-up over autoregressive decoding, while remaining within 3% of the target model’s accuracy on reasoning, instruction-following, and coding benchmarks.

## 1 Introduction 

Autoregressive generation from neural language models is inherently sequential: each token depends on all previous tokens, so generating a single sequence requires as many serial forward passes through the model as there are tokens in the output. This sequential bottleneck is the primary obstacle to faster inference.

K-tokens, and the larger target model veri-fies all K tokens in a single forward pass, ac-cepting a prefix and rejecting the rest. This rejection criterion ensures that the result-ing strings are distributed exactly according to the target, while the speed-up factor is stochastic, depending on the alignment of the draft and target models. This paper takes a fundamentally differ-ent approach to accelerating autoregressive generation. The key modification is replacing SD’s token-level re-jection step with importance-weighted resampling, making the verification step an instance of sequential Monte Carlo (SMC; Doucet et al., 2001; Del Moral et al., 2006; Naesseth et al., 2024). Our method reverses the guarantee of SD—approximation quality is stochastic and the speed-up factor is deterministic. We call our method sequential Monte Carlo speculative decoding (SMC-SD) and describe it concretely in § 3. We derive a closed-form expression for the tokens-per-second (TPS) speed-up factor of SMC-SD with the roofline model (Williams et al., 2009) of the GPU in § 3.1. We show that the roofline model gives a simple characterization of the speed-up factor in both the memory bandwidth-bound and compute-bound regimes. From a theory perspective, because SMC-SD is ultimately based on importance resampling, non-asymptotic bounds on its approximation error can be obtained using known techniques. In the case of SMC-SD, we show that both the L2 bias and mean squared error decay linearly in the number of particles and the L1 bias decays in the root of the number of particles with constants governed by the χ2-divergence between draft and target model (§ 3.2). From a systems perspective, SMC-SD presents a novel computation pattern that necessitates designing an inference engine around it. In § 3.3, we use several observations at the intersection of hardware and algorithm to guide the design of our high-throughput SMC-SD inference engine. Both the drafting and verifying stages of SMC-SD use a vectorizable execution structure that map naturally onto GPU hardware and enable greater parallelism than standard speculative decoding. For KV cache management, we observe that all KV cache data movement during the resampling step can be replaced with efficient in-place pointer exchanges, and we reduce the size of the cache by leveraging the shared prefix among particles. Empirically, SMC-SD achieves up to 2.5 × the throughput of an optimized implementation of speculative decoding on GSM8K (Cobbe et al., 2021), MATH500 (Lightman et al., 2023), AlpacaEval (Dubois et al., 2025), and DS1000 (Lai et al., 2022) across both Llama (Team, 2024) and Qwen (Yang et al., 2025) model families, while remaining within 3% of the target model’s accuracy. In a multi-GPU setting, SMC-SD achieves a 2.36 × speed-up over state-of-the-art speculative decoding and 5.2 × over the autoregressive baseline. Unlike SD, no token-level accept/reject chain truncates a draft midway; every particle is extended by exactly K tokens per round (Figure 2). Each particle independently drafts K sequential tokens from its own current prefix under 

q, then the target model p scores all N sequences in a single batched forward pass and generates a bonus token. The resulting block weights update the particle population, and a low-ESS resampling step duplicates high-weight trajectories while pruning low-weight ones. The round updates the internal particle population block by block; after the final round, one complete sequence is sampled from the terminal normalized weights and returned as the model output. That is, SMC-SD maintains latent particle trajectories during generation and samples the returned sequence only at termination. 

3.1 Speed-up Analysis 

We analyze SMC-SD TPS under the roofline model (Williams et al., 2009), which classifies a workload according to its arithmetic intensity , or the floating-point operations (FLOPs) used per byte of memory loaded for the workload. Whether a workload is memory bandwidth-bound or compute-bound depends on whether its arithmetic intensity is greater than or less than the ridge point of the hardware, or its peak FLOPs per memory bandwidth. Let 

B denote the number of independent user requests served concurrently (the batch size ), and let the effective batch size denote BN , the total number of particles across all requests. The target model’s forward pass processes BN (K+1) tokens per round: N particles per request, each extended by K draft tokens plus one bonus token. Let c = Tq/Tp ≪ 1 be the draft-to-target decoding-time ratio; under the roofline model, c ≈ ρ def 

= Pq/Pp (assuming the draft and target have the same quantization), since memory-bound latency is proportional to parameter count. Let R denote the effective batch size (in tokens) at which a forward pass reaches the ridge point; this depends only on the hardware and quantization format. Below, we characterize the speed-up factor depending on whether the verification pass is memory-bound or compute-bound (proofs in Lemmas D.3 and D.4).

3.3 High-Throughput SMC-SD Engine Design 

SMC-SD is not merely an algorithm that happens to run on GPUs—its vectorized structure maps onto the batch-parallel execution model of modern accelerators and makes use of modern optimizations on inference engines including dynamic batching, PagedAttention, and RadixAttention (Yu et al., 2022; Kwon et al., 2023; Zheng et al., 2024). As such, we build our SMC-SD engine as a fork of SGLang (Zheng et al., 2024). We describe our high-throughput inference engine for SMC-SD, organized around a series of observations at the intersection of algorithm and hardware. 

Observation 1: SMC-SD converts under-utilized compute into speed. Autoregressive decoding is heavily memory bandwidth-bound: each token requires loading the full model weights yet performs only a single vector–matrix multiply. The arithmetic intensity , the FLOPs performed per byte of memory load, for a forward pass of a language model quantized to b bytes per parameter remains at just AI AR = 2 /b. Standard speculative decoding increases the arithmetic intensity by a factor of K but both methods remain far below the roofline ridge points of modern GPUs—312 for A100, 412 for H200, 584 for B300 in FP16 (NVIDIA Corporation, 2020; 2024b;c;a)—leaving the vast majority of available compute idle. SMC-SD closes this gap by leveraging parallelism along the batch-wise and sequence-wise 

axes. The draft model uses dynamic batching to concurrently generate N tokens at a time in lockstep, followed by the target model scoring a block of N(K+1) tokens using a single re-prefill step. Let P denote the target model’s parameter count, b the bytes per parameter (b=2 for FP16, b=1 for INT8), and ρ = Pq/Pp the draft-to-target parameter ratio. Over a full SMC-SD cycle, the arithmetic intensity is (proofs in § D) AI cycle = 2N(ρK+K+1)

Observation 2: Pointer operations turn prefix-sharing into compute and memory gains. 

The resampling step in SMC-SD introduces a novel challenge in inference engine design. When resampling duplicates a high-weight particle, a na ¨ıve implementation would require KV-cache data movement across particles and re-prefill for the duplicated sequences. In SMC-SD, we avoid both of these costs by exploiting the prefix-sharing structure together with page-based KV-cache management (Kwon et al., 2023) and RadixAttention (Zheng et al., 2024). Specifically, duplicated particles share exactly the same prefix as their ancestors up to the resampling point, so the engine can perform resampling by copying page metadata and incrementing the reference counts rather than copying the underlying KV tensors directly. As a result, resampling is implemented as an O(ℓseq ) page-metadata operation, rather than an O(ℓseq × dmodel ) KV-tensor copy. Exploiting prefix sharing not only avoids KV-cache data movement and reduces FLOPs, but also lowers memory usage. ## 5 Related Work 

SMC for Language Models. Recent work has applied SMC to large language models for constrained generation : steering outputs toward syntactic constraints (Loula et al., 2025; Xefteri et al., 2025), semantic specifications (Lew et al., 2023), or reward-defined posteriors (Zhao et al., 2024). In these settings, the target is a posterior p(x | constraint ) and the proposal is the unconstrained language model. SMC-SD repurposes the same machinery for inference acceleration : the target is p itself and the proposal is a faster draft model q.

Exact speculative decoding. Tree decoding algorithms (Cai et al., 2024; Li et al., 2025b) extend standard SD by proposing a tree of tokens at each position, increasing the expected number of accepted tokens while preserving exact sampling. SMC-SD trades exactness for speed via a tunable approximation. 

Approximate speculative decoding. Several methods relax exactness through heuristic acceptance criteria. FSD (Holsman et al., 2025) accepts tokens based on draft–target diver-gence; FLy (Li et al., 2025a) uses an entropy gate with a deferred acceptance window; Judge Decoding (Bachmann et al., 2025) trains a judge network to replace rejection sampling. All retain a per-token accept/reject gate: a single rejection truncates the block, each token’s contribution is binary, and outputs are variable-length. SMC-SD instead assigns each block acontinuous importance weight, produces a fixed number of tokens per round, and provides asymptotic convergence guarantees—all without additional training. 

Complementary strategies. Several orthogonal techniques can be combined with SMC-SD. On the drafting side, EAGLE (Li et al., 2024; 2025b;c) conditions the draft model on target-model embeddings, Medusa (Cai et al., 2024) attaches parallel decoding heads, and DFlash (Chen et al., 2026) uses a diffusion model—all producing higher-quality or faster proposals 9Preprint. Under review. that would directly benefit SMC-SD. On the scheduling side, Specdec++ (Huang et al., 2025) adaptively modulates speculation length and SSD (Kumar et al., 2026) asynchronously overlaps drafting and verifying; analogous adaptive strategies over N and K are a natural extension of SMC-SD. All results use the optimal temperature parameters for each task for both SMC-SD and SD. 

## 6 Conclusion 

We presented SMC-SD, an algorithm that accelerates LLM inference by replacing speculative decoding’s token-level rejection with importance-weighted resampling over a population of draft particles. We established non-asymptotic error bounds showing that the bias and MSE of the importance sampling component of SMC-SD decays as O(1 /N), and we demonstrated empirically that SMC-SD achieves 2.36 × the throughput of optimized speculative decoding and 5.2 × over autoregressive decoding in a multi-GPU setting, while remaining within 3% of the target model’s accuracy across reasoning, instruction-following, and coding benchmarks. Beyond acceleration, a key property of SMC-SD is that the same importance-weighting machinery can target distributions beyond p itself. Given a factor Ψ, SMC-SD can sample approximately from distributions of the form π(x1: T ) ∝ p(x1: T ) · Ψ(x1: T ), provided one can define a sequence of potential functions {ψt}Tt=1 from which properly weighted pairs 

(wt, xt) ∼ ψt can be drawn (Del Moral et al., 2006). The usual SMC convergence guarantees carry over. Three natural instantiations are: • Power sampling (Ψ = pα−1, targeting π ∝ pα). Unlike per-token temperature scaling, this tempers the distribution over entire sequences . § F shows a 10% Pass@1 improvement on MATH500 with Llama-1B → 8B and a dominating Pass@ k for k > 1. • Constrained generation (Ψ = 1[x ∈ A ]). Hard constraints—syntactic validity, format compliance, length bounds—can be enforced while preserving the target’s distribution conditioned on the constraint. Prior work (Lew et al., 2023; Loula et al., 2025; Xefteri et al., 2025) has shown that such constraints can improve accuracy on coding bench-marks; incorporating them into SMC-SD could simultaneously increase downstream performance while accelerating inference. • Reward-weighted decoding (Ψ = exp (β · R(x)) , targeting π ∝ p · eβR). This targets the KL-regularized optimal policy arising in RLHF without fine-tuning, which is relevant for agentic settings where the same base model must be steered toward different objectives. Standard speculative decoding cannot express any of these: it requires knowledge of the normalizing constant of the target, which is unavailable for this class of distributions. On the systems side, much work remains to achieve the theoretical roofline model speedup of SMC-SD demonstrated in Figure 3. First, there are opportunities to overlap resampling with drafting to remove its performance overhead altogether. This can be done by pre-emptively speculating the result of the ESS threshold, and beginning the next round of drafting accordingly. Furthermore, the draft and target models can be disaggregated across hardware, and asynchronously dispatch drafting and scoring calls. Second, by allowing 

N and K to vary dynamically, SMC-SD can adapt to underlying hardware and workload characteristics, enabling runtime exploration of its tunable Pareto frontier. Finally, SMC-SD reflects a trend in co-designing inference algorithms with asymmetric hardware scaling— compute grows faster than memory bandwidth (Zadouri et al., 2026). For instance, the Blackwell GPU architecture significantly increases FLOPS over the previous generation of Hopper GPU and keeps memory bandwidth approximately equal; this increased compute budget makes it an ideal target for SMC-SD. 10 Preprint. ## D Hardware Utilization and Speedup Proofs 

Let P denote the target model’s parameter count, b the bytes per parameter, ρ = Pq/Pp the draft-to-target parameter ratio, c = Tq/Tp ≪ 1 the draft-to-target decoding-time ratio. Let 

B denote the number of independent user requests served concurrently (the batch size ), and let BN denote the effective batch size —the total number of particles across all requests. In the memory-bound regime c ≈ ρ. In one SMC-SD step, the draft model runs K sequential forward passes (one per token position, with all BN particles batched), taking cT pK seconds. The target model scores all BN (K+1) tokens (including bonus tokens) in a single forward pass. The resampling step is negligible.

## E Speed-up Under Declining Acceptance Rate 

Standard SD’s throughput is tightly coupled to the draft–target acceptance rate, which drops as sampling temperature increases (or more generally, as the draft and target distributions diverge). SMC-SD, by contrast, always outputs K+1 tokens per round regardless of accep-tance, so its throughput is largely temperature-invariant.
