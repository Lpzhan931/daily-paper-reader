## MM-M ATRYOSHKA : Towards Budget-Elastic Visual Document Retrieval via a 2D Multimodal Matryoshka Training Framework 

Haowen Xiang 1, Yibo Yan 1,2,3, Jiahao Huo 1,2, Yu Huang 1,2, Yi Cao 2, Mingdong Ou 2, Xuming Hu 1,3*

Abstract 

Multi-vector visual document retrievers achieve strong fine-grained matching by representing each page with multiple vectors from deep Vision-Language Models (VLMs), but this design makes deployment expensive in both storage and computational overhead .Existing efficiency techniques usually optimize only part of this budget, leaving multimodal retrievers without a unified way to trade accu-racy for both vector width and encoder depth .Therefore, we propose MM-M ATRYOSHKA ,a 2D Matryoshka training framework for budget-elastic Visual Document Retrieval (VDR) , enabling ColPali-style multi-vector retrieval elastic along both dimension and layer. At inference time, a single retriever can select a 2D selectable budget without training separate models for different budgets. Through comprehensive experiments across multiple representative backbones, we demonstrate that by retaining significantly higher quality than direct truncation baselines while substantially reducing storage and computational overhead, MM-M ATRYOSHKA can offer robust budget elasticity for efficient VDR. 

1 Introduction 

VDR aims to retrieve relevant document pages given natural-language queries while preserving ev-idence from text, layout, tables, charts, and visual regions (Yan et al., 2026a). Recent ColPali-style systems (Faysse et al., 2025) adapt VLMs to this setting with late interaction (Khattab and Zaharia, 2020): queries and pages are encoded into multiple vectors, and relevance is computed by matching each query vector to its most similar page vector. Though effective, such a design is costly in de-ployment, as the machine must store many vectors.

Related multimodal retrieval work also explores flexible interaction budgets for multi-modal retrieval (Xiao et al., 2025). This direction is especially relevant for VDR because multi-vector pages multiply the storage of every retained di-mension or token. Yet existing multimodal MRL-related compression still leaves a major source of cost untouched: the retriever typically relies on final-layer representations from a VLM encoder .Therefore, we propose MM-M ATRYOSHKA , a 2D matryoshka training framework, which aims to make both the vector width and the encoder depth budget elastic in the VDR domain , as il-lustrated in Figure 1 (c). Specifically, dimension-level MRL loss makes multiple vector prefixes di-rectly retrievable, and layer-level MRL loss trans-fers final-layer retrieval behavior to sampled inter-mediate layers. This design jointly targets storage and encoder latency through d and ℓ, while retaining the fine-grained matching behavior that makes VDR accurate. ❶ We are the first to formulate a 2D training frame-work MM-M ATRYOSHKA for VDR, enabling a single retriever to balance efficiency and accu-racy across both vector width and encoder depth. 

❷ MM-M ATRYOSHKA is robust across represen-tative VLM backbones, achieving the expected budget-elastic behavior through improved low-dimensional and intermediate-layer retention. 

❸ We conduct a comprehensive experimental val-idation across 14 VDR datasets, covering both performance and efficiency, and complement the quantitative results with qualitative case studies. 

2 Related Work 

2.1 Visual Document Retrieval 

VDR retrieves document pages from the multi-modal corpus rather than from extracted text alone (Osmulski et al., 2025; Yan et al., 2026d). Col-Pali (Faysse et al., 2025) showed that VLMs can be adapted to this setting with late interaction, mak-ing it possible to match query vectors against fine-grained page regions. ViDoRe team (Macé et al., 2025; Faysse et al., 2025) further provides bench-marks for evaluating visual retrieval across diverse document domains. In parallel, universal multi-modal embedding models have advanced VDR with single-vector representations (Jiang et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2024; Chen et al., 2025; Günther et al., 2025; Xu et al., 2025). Recent multi-vector 

systems such as Llama-Nemoretriever-Colembed (Xu et al., 2025) and Nemotron ColEmbed V2 (Moreira et al., 2026) maintain top-tier accuracy but severely exacerbate storage bottlenecks by de-manding thousands of high-dimensional embed-dings per document. MM-M ATRYOSHKA focuses on a critical efficiency question in the VDR domain: 

for multi-vector VDR systems that intentionally keep many vectors, can the same retriever expose a lower-cost selectable budget? 

2.2 Efficient Multi-Vector Retrieval 

Efficient multi-vector retrieval aims to preserve the accuracy benefits of late interaction while miti-gating its prohibitive storage footprint and search latency (Lee et al., 2023; Archish et al., 2026; Shrestha et al., 2024). In the textual domain , foun-dational works like ColBERTv2 (Santhanam et al., 2022b) and PLAID (Santhanam et al., 2022a) uti-lize residual compression and centroid-based prun-ing, while CITADEL (Li et al., 2023) routes token vectors through dynamic lexical keys. Recently, the severe memory bottleneck of VLMs has spurred a wave of efficiency optimizations specifically tai-lored for VDR. These multimodal efforts primar-ily focus on token reduction along the sequence dimension: pruning-based methods like DocPruner (Yan et al., 2025, 2026c; Bach, 2025) adaptively discard redundant visual patches based on attention scores, whereas merging-based frameworks such as Light-ColPali (Ma et al., 2025) and ColChunk (Yan et al., 2026b) aggregate dense patch embeddings via semantic or layout-aware clustering. Further-more, advanced hybrid approaches attempt to com-press indices through learnable summary tokens or attention-guided grouping (Xiao et al., 2025; Qin et al., 2026; Kim et al., 2025; Huo et al., 2026). While these approaches successfully reduce the to-ken count or optimize the retrieval engine, they still operate at a fixed representation scale . MM-MATRYOSHKA offers a fundamentally distinct and complementary paradigm: rather than solely op-timizing token quantity, we empower a single re-triever to be naturally budget-elastic along both vector width and encoder layer depth .

2.3 Matryoshka Representation Learning 

MRL trains embeddings whose prefix dimensions remain useful under different computational bud-gets (Kusupati et al., 2022). Recent work adapts this idea to existing LLM embeddings and retrieval-oriented embedding compression (Yoon et al., 2024; Zhang et al., 2025). More closely related to our budget formulation, 2D Matryoshka meth-ods expose a joint space over embedding width and Transformer layer depth for sentence embed-dings (Li et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2025; Zhuang et al., 2024). MRL has also been explored in VLMs by controlling visual-token granularity rather than retrieval-vector width or encoder depth (Cai et al., 2025). However, most existing studies are based on text retrieval or general representation learning (Lai et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2024b; Huy et al., 2026; Wang et al., 2026; Bussmann et al., 2025). Multi-vector VDR introduces a different constraint: compression must preserve many vectors-to-region alignments used by late interaction (Faysse et al., 2025). We therefore investigate 2D Matryoshka training specifically in VDR, where the goal is not only smaller embeddings but a 2D budget space. 

3 Methodology 

We adapt the MRL idea from single-vector sen-tence embeddings (Li et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2025; Zhuang et al., 2024) to multi-vector VDR. Instead of producing one sentence vector Xdn from layer n and dimension d, a VDR model produces many query and page token vectors, and retrieval quality is determined by a late-interaction score ma-trix. The goal of MM-M ATRYOSHKA is therefore to make the score matrix reliable under a selected lower encoder layer ℓ and a reduced prefix dimen-sion d, as illustrated in Figure 2.

The target layer set S and prefix set D are chosen according to the characteristics of each backbone, including its encoder depth, retrieval-head width, and the avail-ability of useful intermediate hidden states. Dimension reduc-tion directly reduces token-vector storage and dot-product width. Layer reduction uses hidden states from an earlier layer; with an early-exit implemen-tation, it can also reduce encoder computation.

4.1.3 Efficiency-Oriented Budget Evaluation 

We evaluate efficiency as a quality–cost trade-off over selectable 2D budgets. For retrieval quality, each trained checkpoint is evaluated at budget (ℓ, d )

by selecting the representation from a target layer ℓ.We evaluate different target-layer sets depending on the backbone, together with the shared Matryoshka dimension set. For measured deployment efficiency, we report a PaliGemma study on the same datasets using the same trained checkpoint. The measured selected grid combines the target layer and dimension set, using the final-layer full-width budget as the refer-ence. We organize the efficiency protocol around three deployment costs. Indexing–storage mea-sures the bf16 page-vector footprint after compres-sion, computed as the number of page tokens times

5 Conclusion 

In this work, we propose MM-M ATRYOSHKA , a novel 2D training framework designed to over-come the prohibitive storage and computational bottlenecks of multi-vector VDR. Unlike existing compression techniques that operate at a fixed rep-resentation scale, our approach empowers a sin-gle retriever to be naturally budget-elastic along both encoder layer depth and embedding vector width. By jointly optimizing dimension-level pre-fix supervision and layer-wise score distillation, MM-M ATRYOSHKA allows practitioners to dy-namically select a 2D inference budget tailored to specific latency and storage constraints. Extensive experiments demonstrate that MM-M ATRYOSHKA 

gracefully balances efficiency and accuracy, far bet-ter than direct truncation baselines. Ultimately, MM-M ATRYOSHKA establishes a foundational paradigm for resource-constrained deployment, paving the way for the next generation of scalable multimodal retrieval systems. 

Limitations 

While MM-M ATRYOSHKA successfully estab-lishes a robust budget-elastic paradigm for efficient VDR, there remain a few promising avenues for further exploration: • Hardware-aware Deployment Optimiza-tion: Although our framework theoretically supports depth-level elasticity, achieving op-timal end-to-end inference speedups across diverse specialized hardware requires bespoke system-level engineering for early-exit execu-tion. We plan to develop custom deployment configurations and integrate them into popular inference engines ( e.g., TensorRT or vLLM) to fully actualize these hardware-aware la-tency gains in industrial settings. • Dynamic Budget Allocation: The current methodology relies on a user-defined, stati-cally selected 2D budget at inference time, rather than dynamically adjusting the compu-tation based on the inherent complexity of individual queries or document pages. In future work, we aim to design an instance-aware routing mechanism that automatically predicts and assigns the minimal sufficient layer-dimension pair for each sample without sacrificing overall accuracy. • Extension to Temporal Modalities: Our em-pirical scope is currently dedicated to spatial document images, leaving the framework’s adaptability to continuous temporal modal-ities unverified. We intend to expand this 2D Matryoshka paradigm to video-language models to investigate whether temporal frame-level elasticity can be seamlessly incorporated into our joint budget space.

Each trained retriever is evaluated against the cor-responding public late-interaction baseline: Col-Pali, ColQwen2, and ColQwen2.5. All reported compression results are obtained from the same checkpoint per backbone by selecting different 2D budgets at inference time, without training separate models for each budget. 

Datasets and metric scale. The ViDoRe v1 aver-ages use ten datasets: docvqa_test_subsampled ,

infovqa_test_subsampled , tabfquad_test_ subsampled , arxivqa_test_subsampled ,

tatdqa_test , shiftproject_test ,

syntheticDocQA_artificial_intelligence_ test , syntheticDocQA_energy_test ,

syntheticDocQA_government_reports_test ,and syntheticDocQA_healthcare_industry_ test . The ViDoRe v2 averages use four datasets: 

esg_reports_v2 , biomedical_lectures_v2 ,

economics_reports_v2 , and esg_reports_ human_labeled_v2 . Table values report nDCG@5 multiplied by 100 . The Qwen2-VL ablation table uses a common three-dataset ViDoRe v2 subset for which all ablation variants completed: ESG reports, biomedical lectures, and human-labeled ESG reports. 

Layer and dimension probes. The main compression-retention tables report three layer bud-gets for each backbone: full, middle, and shallow. These correspond to {18 , 9, 4, 2} for PaliGemma, 

{28 , 14 , 7} for Qwen2-VL, and {36 , 18 , 9} for Qwen2.5-VL. All three backbone families use the common prefix dimensions {128 , 64 , 32 }.

Baseline truncation. For public baselines and trained models, lower-dimensional evaluation uses the same protocol: slice the prefix dimensions of every query and document token vector, re-normalize the sliced vectors, and score with the same late-interaction MaxSim function. The re-ported prefix dimensions are shared across back-bones, so the compression-retention comparison uses the same {128 , 64 , 32 } dimension grid for PaliGemma, Qwen2-VL, and Qwen2.5-VL.
